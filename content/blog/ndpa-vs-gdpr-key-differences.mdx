# NDPA vs GDPR: Key Differences for International Businesses Operating in Nigeria

If your business serves customers in both Nigeria and the European Union, you are subject to two data protection laws: the Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023 and the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Many business owners assume that GDPR compliance automatically covers them for Nigeria. It does not.

While the NDPA draws significant inspiration from the GDPR, there are important differences in scope, enforcement, and specific requirements. Getting these wrong means you are non-compliant in Nigeria regardless of how buttoned-up your EU operations are.

Here are the 10 key differences every international business needs to understand.

## 1. Regulatory Bodies

**GDPR:** Enforced by Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) in each EU member state. If your business operates across multiple EU countries, you may deal with several regulators, though the "one-stop-shop" mechanism designates a lead authority.

**NDPA:** Enforced by a single body — the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC). All complaints, registrations, and enforcement actions go through one commission. This is simpler in one sense, but it also means there is no regulatory arbitrage. You deal with the NDPC, full stop.

## 2. Territorial Scope

**GDPR:** Applies to any organisation processing data of EU residents, regardless of where the organisation is based. A Nigerian company selling to EU customers is subject to the GDPR.

**NDPA:** Applies to any organisation processing personal data of data subjects in Nigeria, or processing data within Nigeria. An EU company with Nigerian customers or a Nigerian office is subject to the NDPA.

The practical implication: if you operate in both markets, you need to comply with both laws independently. There is no mutual recognition arrangement.

## 3. Lawful Bases for Processing

**GDPR:** Six lawful bases — consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and legitimate interests.

**NDPA:** Also six lawful bases under Section 25, largely mirroring the GDPR. However, the interpretation and application differ. The NDPC's guidance on legitimate interest, for instance, may evolve differently from EU case law on the same concept.

Businesses should not assume that a legitimate interest assessment conducted for GDPR purposes will satisfy the NDPA. Document your [lawful basis](/ndpr-demos/lawful-basis) analysis separately for each jurisdiction.

## 4. Breach Notification Timelines

**GDPR:** 72 hours to notify the supervisory authority after becoming aware of a breach.

**NDPA:** Also 72 hours to notify the NDPC under Section 40.

While the headline deadline is the same, the requirements for what must be included in the notification differ. The NDPA requires specific information about measures taken to address the breach and mitigate its effects. If you operate in both jurisdictions and suffer a breach affecting both EU and Nigerian data subjects, you need to file separate notifications with different content to different authorities within the same 72-hour window.

## 5. Cross-Border Data Transfer Mechanisms

**GDPR:** Transfers outside the EU require adequacy decisions, Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs), or other approved safeguards.

**NDPA:** Section 43 restricts transfers outside Nigeria unless the destination country provides an adequate level of protection or appropriate safeguards are in place. The NDPC maintains its own adequacy determinations, which are separate from and do not automatically align with EU adequacy decisions.

A country deemed adequate by the EU may not be deemed adequate by the NDPC, and vice versa. Businesses transferring data between Nigeria and EU need to ensure they have valid transfer mechanisms in place for both directions.

## 6. Penalties Comparison

**GDPR:** Up to 4% of annual global turnover or 20 million Euros, whichever is greater, for the most serious violations.

**NDPA:** Up to 2% of annual gross revenue or 10 million Naira (approximately 6,000 Euros at current rates), whichever is greater.

The GDPR penalties are significantly higher in absolute terms. However, for businesses with operations primarily in Nigeria, the NDPA penalties are proportionally meaningful — 2% of gross revenue is a substantial hit for any business, regardless of size.

## 7. Data Subject Rights

**GDPR:** Eight rights — access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, automated decision-making, and withdrawal of consent.

**NDPA:** Eight rights under Sections 29-36, closely mirroring the GDPR. However, the procedural requirements for responding to data subject requests differ. The NDPA sets its own timelines and conditions for when you can refuse a request.

If your business receives a [data subject request](/ndpr-demos/dsr) from a Nigerian customer, you must handle it under NDPA rules, not GDPR rules. The response deadlines and exemptions are not identical.

## 8. Data Protection Officer (DPO) Requirements

**GDPR:** A DPO is required for public authorities, organisations engaged in large-scale systematic monitoring, or organisations processing special categories of data at scale.

**NDPA:** The NDPA mandates DPOs for certain categories of data controllers, with the specific thresholds defined by the NDPC. The criteria are not identical to the GDPR's, so a business that does not need a DPO under GDPR may still need one under the NDPA, or vice versa.

## 9. Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)

**GDPR:** Required when processing is likely to result in a high risk to individuals' rights and freedoms.

**NDPA:** Also required for high-risk processing under the NDPA, but the definition of "high-risk" may differ from GDPR interpretations. The NDPC issues its own guidance on when a [DPIA](/ndpr-demos/dpia) is required.

## 10. Registration and Filing Requirements

**GDPR:** No general registration requirement with supervisory authorities (though some member states have specific requirements).

**NDPA:** The NDPC requires data controllers and processors to register, particularly those processing data above certain thresholds. This is an obligation that has no direct equivalent in the GDPR, and international businesses often overlook it.

## Why GDPR Compliance Alone Is Not Enough

The bottom line is that GDPR and NDPA are separate legal frameworks with separate regulators, separate enforcement mechanisms, and separate requirements. Relying on your GDPR compliance programme to cover Nigeria is like relying on your Nigerian tax filing to cover your EU tax obligations — the logic does not hold.

You need to:

- Conduct a separate NDPA compliance assessment
- Document your lawful bases under NDPA Section 25 (not just GDPR Article 6)
- Register with the NDPC if required
- Ensure your cross-border transfer mechanisms are valid under Section 43
- Train your team on NDPA-specific requirements
